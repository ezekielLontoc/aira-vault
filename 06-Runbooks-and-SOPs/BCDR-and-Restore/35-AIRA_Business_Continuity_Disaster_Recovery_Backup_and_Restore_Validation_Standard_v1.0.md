# 1. Executive Summary

AIRA is a mission-critical, AI-native enterprise platform. Its ability to operate safely is not proven only by successful deployment; it is proven by the ability to continue, recover, restore, reconcile, and explain the system after infrastructure failure, data corruption, cyber incident, configuration error, application defect, model-routing failure, human error, or business disruption.

This standard defines the mandatory Business Continuity, Disaster Recovery, Backup, and Restore Validation discipline for AIRA. It converts continuity from a general IT intention into an evidence-producing engineering control covering RTO/RPO ownership, backup design, restore tests, failover, failback, dependency mapping, immutable evidence, Zammad ticketing, Wazuh SIEM, TheHive/Cortex case handling, SigNoz/APM traces, and AVCI-aligned improvement.

| Core Rule<br>AIRA does not accept “backup completed” as sufficient evidence. The required proof is “restore completed, integrity verified, application validated, business owner accepted, evidence retained, and improvement actions tracked.” |

## 1.1 What This Standard Establishes

AIRA continuity-tier model with mandatory RTO/RPO ownership and approval.

Backup, retention, immutability, encryption, access, monitoring, and chain-of-custody requirements.

Restore validation procedure for databases, document stores, Kubernetes workloads, GitOps state, secrets, AI registries, observability, ITSM, and security platforms.

Failover and failback governance with go/no-go gates, rollback path, stakeholder communication, and post-recovery review.

Evidence templates for backup jobs, restore tests, DR drills, BIA records, dependency maps, and recovery exceptions.

Controls for Wazuh, TheHive, Cortex, SigNoz, Zammad, OpenTelemetry, Prometheus, Loki, Tempo, Grafana, and AIRA audit evidence.

AVCI-aligned improvement loop so every failed test, drill gap, or incident finding updates the runbook, architecture, backlog, or ADR/TDL.

## 1.2 What This Standard Is Not

It is not a generic corporate BCP policy. It is the AIRA engineering and operational recovery standard.

It is not a substitute for business-owner continuity procedures. It defines the technology recovery control baseline that business continuity depends on.

It is not an authorization to perform production failover without approval. Emergency actions remain governed by CAB, incident command, and security policy.

It is not limited to infrastructure backups. It covers documents, data, metadata, prompts, model aliases, guardrails, evidence, tickets, observability, and security cases.

# 2. Purpose, Scope, and Authority

## 2.1 Purpose

The purpose of this standard is to ensure that AIRA can sustain or restore mission-critical operations within approved business tolerances and prove that capability through tested evidence. The standard establishes minimum requirements for business impact analysis, dependency registration, backup configuration, restore validation, DR drill execution, failover/failback governance, evidence retention, and continuous improvement.

## 2.2 In Scope

AIRA application services, APIs, MicroFunction runtime, workflows, schedulers, workers, and integration services.

Infrastructure and platform layers: Linux, Proxmox, Kubernetes, container registry, GitOps, network/service mesh, ingress, and storage.

Data platforms: PostgreSQL/Patroni, pgvector, PgBouncer, OpenKM, object storage, Redis/Valkey, Kafka, Temporal, Flowable, Keycloak, Vault/OpenBao, and related metadata stores.

AI-native artifacts: prompt templates, guardrails, model aliases, evaluation datasets, embeddings, retrieval indexes, LightRAG graphs, LLM Wiki, Obsidian canonical notes, AI evidence, and model-routing configuration.

Operational platforms: Zammad, Wazuh, TheHive, Cortex, SigNoz, Prometheus, Thanos, Loki, Tempo, Grafana Alloy, Grafana, Sentry, OpenTelemetry collectors, and alerting rules.

Recovery evidence, DR exercises, BIA records, dependency maps, recovery exceptions, waivers, and post-incident/post-drill improvement records.

## 2.3 Out of Scope

Corporate-wide crisis management unrelated to AIRA platform recovery.

Physical building, safety, and facilities continuity, except where these affect AIRA infrastructure availability.

Business-user manual workarounds in detail; these are owned by business process continuity plans and referenced by this standard.

Vendor contractual SLA negotiation, except where SLA terms affect AIRA recovery assumptions and must be recorded.

# 3. BCP/DR Governance Principles

| ID | Principle | Operational Meaning |

| --- | --- | --- |

| BCP-01 | Continuity is business-owned and technology-enabled | Business process owners define criticality and acceptable downtime; IT designs and proves recovery capability. |

| BCP-02 | RTO/RPO must be explicit | Every critical service, data store, evidence repository, and operational tool has an approved RTO and RPO. |

| BCP-03 | Restore is the control, backup is the input | A backup is incomplete until restored, integrity-checked, security-checked, and accepted. |

| BCP-04 | No untested recovery path | Runbooks, scripts, replicas, snapshots, and backups require scheduled exercise evidence. |

| BCP-05 | GitOps rebuild over manual reconstruction | Runtime state is rebuilt from approved source where possible; manual reconstruction is a controlled exception. |

| BCP-06 | Data integrity before availability | AIRA must not restore corrupted, stale, unauthorized, or misclassified data simply to meet uptime pressure. |

| BCP-07 | Classified recovery handling | Confidential and Restricted artifacts require controlled access, redaction, audit, and approved restore targets. |

| BCP-08 | Immutable recovery evidence | DR drill logs, restore reports, approvals, and exceptions are retained under the evidence register. |

| BCP-09 | No shadow backup path | Unmanaged exports, personal copies, desktop archives, screenshots, and private cloud folders are prohibited as recovery sources. |

| BCP-10 | AVCI always | Every continuity artifact must be attributable, verifiable, classifiable, and improvable. |

# 4. Criticality, RTO/RPO, and Service Tiering

Continuity requirements must be agreed with business owners, Security, Compliance, Operations, and Architecture. The values below are planning baselines. Actual targets must be approved per business process, environment, and production release.

| Tier | Typical Scope | Planning RTO | Planning RPO | Mandatory Controls |

| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

| Tier 0 - Mission Critical | Core transaction processing, identity, secrets, PostgreSQL system of record, OpenKM core records, workflow history, audit evidence. | <= 2 hours or approved business target | <= 15 minutes or approved business target | HA design, PITR/WAL or equivalent, tested failover, encrypted immutable backup, quarterly restore test, annual full DR exercise. |

| Tier 1 - High Criticality | Gateway, workflow services, Kafka integration, Zammad, Wazuh/TheHive, CI/CD release evidence, model-routing registry. | <= 4 hours | <= 1 hour | Automated backup, documented rebuild path, restore test at least semi-annual, failover or rebuild runbook. |

| Tier 2 - Important | Reporting, non-critical admin services, curated knowledge projections, dashboards, non-production support services. | <= 1 business day | <= 4 hours | Scheduled backup or GitOps rebuild, annual restore test, documented owner. |

| Tier 3 - Rebuildable / Derivative | Redis/Valkey cache, derived indexes, pgvector ANN index, LightRAG graph projections, temporary runtime state. | Rebuild within agreed window | Source-derived; no independent RPO | Must be rebuildable from Tier-0 source; cache loss must not affect correctness. |

| RTO/RPO Approval Rule<br>No production go-live is allowed if Tier 0 or Tier 1 services lack approved RTO/RPO targets, tested restore evidence, assigned recovery owners, and documented fallback or manual business-continuity procedure. |

# 6. Backup Architecture and Control Requirements

AIRA backup architecture must protect three classes of assets: authoritative state, operational evidence, and rebuild sources. Derivative assets such as cache entries and indexes may be rebuilt from authoritative sources, but the rebuild process and triggers must be tested.

| Control Area | Mandatory Requirement |

| --- | --- |

| Backup ownership | Every backup job has owner, scope, schedule, retention, classification, encryption method, restore target, and evidence path. |

| Encryption | Backups must be encrypted in transit and at rest. Key management must use Vault/OpenBao or approved enterprise key management. |

| Access control | Backup repositories use least privilege, separation of duties, MFA, and monitored administrative access. |

| Immutability | Tier 0 and Tier 1 backup sets require immutable or write-once protection where technically feasible. |

| Air-gap / isolation | At least one recovery copy for critical data must be isolated from normal production credentials and ransomware blast radius. |

| Integrity | Backup jobs must record checksum, manifest, source version, backup ID, timestamp, and completion status. |

| Restore rehearsal | Backups must be restored to an isolated target on a defined cadence and after major changes. |

| Classification handling | Backup media and restore targets inherit the highest classification of the protected data. |

| Legal hold | Legal hold suspends purge and must be checked before backup deletion or evidence disposal. |

| Monitoring | Backup failures must generate alert, Zammad ticket, severity classification, owner assignment, and closure evidence. |

| Backup Type | Use Case | AIRA Position |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Snapshot | Rapid recovery from short-term operational issues. | Useful but not sufficient alone. Snapshots must not replace independent backups. |

| Full backup | Baseline recovery point. | Required for Tier 0/Tier 1 systems on approved cadence. |

| Incremental / differential | Efficient recovery between full backups. | Allowed when restore sequence is tested and documented. |

| Continuous archive / WAL / PITR | Database point-in-time recovery. | Required for Tier 0 PostgreSQL where business RPO requires it. |

| GitOps source rebuild | Recreate desired platform state. | Preferred for Kubernetes manifests, policies, prompts, guardrails, and configuration. |

| Export / logical backup | Schema/config portability and audit review. | Allowed as supplemental backup, not the only recovery path for critical systems. |

# 7. Restore Validation Standard

Restore validation is the core control. Restore validation must prove that data can be recovered, applications can read and write safely, security policies remain intact, evidence is retained, and business users can confirm process correctness.

Figure 2. Restore validation flow.

| Validation Stage | Required Test | Evidence Required |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Scope selection | Select backup set, system, environment, data class, and recovery objective. | Restore test plan, owner, backup ID, system scope. |

| Isolated restore | Restore to isolated non-production or DR target without weakening access controls. | Restore job log, target environment ID, access approvals. |

| Integrity check | Verify checksum, counts, schema version, object/document hashes, and metadata consistency. | Integrity report, variance log, DBA/SRE sign-off. |

| Security check | Confirm ACLs, RLS, secrets references, encryption, classification, and audit logging. | Security validation checklist, policy test output. |

| Application check | Run smoke tests, API health checks, representative workflows, and error-path checks. | Test report, traces, logs, CI/manual validation evidence. |

| Business validation | Business owner validates sample transactions, documents, reports, or workflow states. | Business sign-off, exceptions, accepted limitations. |

| Evidence closure | Store evidence and open remediation tickets for gaps. | OpenKM path, Zammad ticket, ADR/TDL or backlog refs. |

| Restore Evidence Rule<br>Restore test evidence must include backup ID, restore target, actor, approver, timestamps, command/job references, integrity result, security result, application result, business result, exceptions, and improvement actions. |

# 8. Disaster Recovery Architecture and Patterns

AIRA DR architecture must be designed around recoverable authority, not uncontrolled replication. The system should favour deterministic rebuild from GitOps and source-of-truth data restore, while using replication and HA only where business RTO/RPO requires it.

| Pattern | Use When | AIRA Governance Rule |

| --- | --- | --- |

| High Availability | Short outages are unacceptable and active node failure must be absorbed quickly. | HA does not replace backup. Failover must be monitored and tested. |

| Warm standby | Tier 0/Tier 1 systems need faster recovery than rebuild-only allows. | Standby must be patched, tested, secured, and evidence-producing. |

| Cold standby / rebuild | Tier 2 systems can tolerate longer recovery. | Runbooks and source artifacts must be sufficient to rebuild within RTO. |

| Point-in-time restore | Database corruption or bad release requires recovery to a known-good point. | Requires WAL/archive evidence, restoration rehearsal, and business reconciliation. |

| Blue/green or canary rollback | Application release defects can be isolated without data rollback. | Use only when database compatibility and forward-fix strategy are confirmed. |

| Manual business continuity | Temporary business processing can continue outside system during outage. | Manual workarounds must be reconciled into AIRA with audit evidence after recovery. |

# 9. Failover, Failback, and Controlled Recovery Procedures

Figure 3. Failover and failback control flow.

| Phase | Required Activities | Decision Owner |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Detect and declare | Alert triage, incident classification, affected services, business impact, security suspicion, evidence preservation. | Incident Commander / Operations Lead |

| Assess recovery option | Choose restart, rollback, restore, failover, degraded operation, manual workaround, or security containment. | Solutions Architect + SRE + Security |

| Authorize | Obtain go/no-go approval based on severity, data risk, customer impact, and regulatory implications. | IT Head / CAB / Executive as needed |

| Execute failover or restore | Follow runbook, record commands/jobs, preserve evidence, avoid uncontrolled manual mutation. | Recovery Owner |

| Validate service | Run technical, security, and business validation; confirm RTO/RPO outcome. | QA / SRE / Business Owner |

| Failback | Plan return to primary site or steady state; reconcile data; confirm no split-brain or duplicate effects. | Operations Lead + DBA + Business Owner |

| Post-review | Document lessons learned, update runbooks, create backlog items, capture audit evidence. | Incident Commander + Internal Audit |

| Failback Rule<br>Failback is a separate controlled change. Do not automatically fail back simply because the primary environment is available. Confirm data integrity, traffic routing, workflow state, event lag, secrets validity, and business owner approval first. |

# 13. AI-Native Auto-Heal, Auto-Learn, and Auto-Improve Controls

AIRA may use AI-assisted analysis to accelerate continuity operations, but AI must not become an uncontrolled recovery actor. The same SOLID enforcement, security, evidence, reversibility, and human-in-the-loop principles apply during DR as during normal development.

| Capability | Allowed Use | Prohibited Use |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Auto-Heal | Detect likely cause, collect evidence, propose runbook step, open Zammad ticket, draft remediation PR, run non-production checks. | Autonomous production failover, database restore, backup purge, secret rotation, or policy override without approval. |

| Auto-Learn | Convert validated recovery lessons into candidate runbook updates, guardrail tests, knowledge entries, and training material. | Promote unreviewed incident content or Restricted data into LLM Wiki or prompt examples. |

| Auto-Improve | Recommend improved backup schedules, monitoring rules, tests, RTO/RPO adjustments, or architecture changes through ADR/TDL. | Self-modify recovery automation, weaken controls, bypass CAB, or reduce evidence retention. |

| AutoResearch | Compare recovery patterns, vendor advisories, security alerts, and technology options under controlled research workflow. | Use unverified external advice directly in production recovery. |

| SOLID / Enterprise Design Principle Rule<br>Auto-Heal, Auto-Learn, and Auto-Improve must preserve or improve SOLID compliance, architecture boundaries, testability, security posture, observability, reversibility, rollbackability, and AVCI evidence. Any candidate that weakens these must be rejected or escalated as a waiver. |

| Metric | Definition | Target / Review Rule |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Backup success rate | Successful backups divided by scheduled backups by tier. | Tier 0/Tier 1 failures generate immediate ticket and management visibility. |

| Restore success rate | Successful restore validations divided by planned restore tests. | Failures create remediation ticket and retest requirement. |

| Actual RTO | Elapsed time from incident declaration or restore start to validated service readiness. | Compare to approved RTO; variance requires root-cause review. |

| Actual RPO | Maximum accepted data loss or replay gap after recovery. | Compare to approved RPO; variance requires business and data-governance approval. |

| Evidence completeness | Percentage of recovery events with complete AVCI evidence fields. | Must improve quarter over quarter; gaps are audit findings. |

| Runbook freshness | Number of runbooks reviewed within cadence. | Expired runbooks block production readiness for the affected component. |

# 17. Acceptance Criteria

All Tier 0 and Tier 1 services have approved RTO/RPO, recovery owner, validation owner, backup method, restore method, and evidence path.

Initial BIA and dependency register exist for MVP and foundation services.

PostgreSQL recovery strategy includes tested backup, WAL/PITR or approved equivalent, and DBA sign-off.

OpenKM document recovery is tested for binary, metadata, version, ACL, and hash integrity.

Vault/OpenBao, Keycloak, Git, container registry, Zammad, Wazuh/TheHive/Cortex, and SigNoz/observability recovery procedures are documented and tested at least once before production go-live.

Kubernetes/GitOps rebuild path is tested for at least one representative service and one environment.

DR alerts create Zammad tickets and security-relevant incidents can be linked to Wazuh/TheHive/Cortex evidence.

Backup and restore evidence is stored in the approved OpenKM evidence path with AVCI metadata.

A tabletop exercise and initial technical restore drill are completed before production acceptance.

All gaps have Zammad tickets, owners, severity, target dates, and management visibility.

# Appendix E. AVCI Compliance Summary

| AVCI Property | Compliance Statement |

| --- | --- |

| Attributable | Every BCP/DR artifact has an owner, recovery owner, validation owner, source system, backup ID, restore test ID, ticket/case reference, and approval path. |

| Verifiable | Backups, restores, failovers, failbacks, and DR drills are verified through logs, checksums, smoke tests, security checks, business validation, metrics, and evidence records. |

| Classifiable | Recovery artifacts inherit source classification and are governed by access, retention, legal hold, model-routing, and storage controls. |

| Improvable | Failed backups, failed restores, missed RTO/RPO, control gaps, incidents, and drill findings create tracked improvement actions through Zammad, ADR/TDL, backlog, or runbook update. |
