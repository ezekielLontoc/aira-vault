# 1.  PURPOSE

This document establishes the BFS Financial Services framework for ensuring that the personnel operating the Information Security Management System (ISMS) are competent, that all personnel are aware of the ISMS and their responsibilities within it, and that information security is communicated effectively across the organization and with relevant external parties. It is the management-system level framework — the strategic and structural framework — within which the operational security awareness and training program (BFS-ISP-014) and the forthcoming HR Security Policy (BFS-ISP-021) deliver execution.

This document exists to fulfill the requirements of ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clauses 7.2, 7.3, and 7.4:

Clause 7.2 (Competence) — requires BFS to determine the necessary competence of persons doing work that affects ISMS performance, ensure these persons are competent on the basis of education, training, or experience, take action to acquire competence where gaps exist, and retain documented information as evidence of competence;

Clause 7.3 (Awareness) — requires BFS to ensure persons doing work under the organization's control are aware of: the information security policy, their contribution to ISMS effectiveness, the implications of not conforming with ISMS requirements, and the benefits of improved information security performance;

Clause 7.4 (Communication) — requires BFS to determine the need for internal and external communications relevant to the ISMS — including on what to communicate, when, with whom, who shall communicate, and the processes by which communication is achieved.

These three clauses are closely related but distinct. Competence is about whether the right people, with the right skills, are doing ISMS work. Awareness is about whether all personnel — not just ISMS workers — understand the ISMS and what they need to do. Communication is about whether information security messages flow effectively to the parties who need them. Together, these three dimensions determine whether the ISMS operates as a living system embedded in the organization or as a paper exercise disconnected from operational reality.

This document is companion to and consistent with BFS-ISP-014 (Security Awareness and Training Policy). Where BFS-ISP-014 specifies the operational training program — courses, frequency, completion tracking, phishing simulations — this document establishes the management-system framework that requires those courses, defines the competencies they must develop, allocates accountability for delivery, and integrates with the broader ISMS governance. The two documents together address ISO 27001 expectations for both Annex A control A.6.3 (operational training) and the Clause 7 management system requirements.

Beyond ISO 27001 compliance, this document fulfills BSP supervisory expectations for IT and cybersecurity workforce capability, RA 10173 personnel competence requirements (particularly the DPO competence expectations of Section 21), PCI DSS v4.0 Requirement 12.6 security awareness obligations, and the broader Philippine financial sector regulatory expectations for fit-and-proper personnel in roles affecting customer information.

The specific objectives of this document are to:

Identify all roles within the BFS ISMS and the necessary competencies for each — using a structured competency framework aligned with NIST NICE Cybersecurity Workforce Framework (SP 800-181);

Define the methods by which BFS verifies competence — pre-employment verification, on-the-job assessment, certification requirements, continuing education obligations;

Mandate awareness coverage for all BFS personnel, with role-based depth — ensuring every person under BFS control understands ISMS implications relevant to their role;

Establish the ISMS communication plan — what is communicated, by whom, to whom, when, and through what mechanism — covering both internal and external communications;

Provide the bridge between ISMS-level requirements (this document) and operational delivery (BFS-ISP-014, BFS-ISP-021, training records, communication artifacts);

Establish KPIs and verification mechanisms ensuring competence, awareness, and communication objectives are measurably achieved;

Provide audit-friendly documentation supporting ISO 27001 certification audits, BSP examinations, NPC compliance verification, and PCI DSS QSA assessments.

# 2.  DEFINITIONS AND SCOPE

## 2.1  Definitions

For the purposes of this document, the following definitions apply. ISO 27001 terminology is controlling where conflict exists with general usage.

| Term | Definition |

| --- | --- |

| Competence | The ability to apply knowledge and skills to achieve intended results in a specific role context. Competence combines three dimensions: knowledge (what one knows), skills (what one can do), and behavioral attributes (how one applies knowledge and skills). Competence is verifiable through education, training, experience, certification, or demonstrated performance. (ISO/IEC 27000:2018) |

| Awareness | The state of knowing about the existence of something and understanding its significance. In ISMS context: knowing about the BFS information security policy, understanding one's responsibilities, knowing the consequences of nonconformity, and recognizing one's contribution to ISMS effectiveness. (ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clause 7.3) |

| Communication | The transmission of information from sender to receiver through defined channels. ISMS communication includes routine operational communication (e.g., security advisories), governance communication (e.g., Board reporting), incident communication (e.g., breach notifications), and crisis communication (e.g., public statements). (ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clause 7.4) |

| Role | A defined position with associated responsibilities, authorities, and accountabilities. ISMS roles include both formal positions (CISO, DPO) and informal but consequential positions (Privacy Champion, Security Champion in business units). |

| Competency Framework | A structured catalog of competencies organized by role and level, supporting consistent assessment, gap analysis, and development planning. BFS uses a tier-based framework aligned with NIST NICE. |

| Education | Formal academic learning typically resulting in degrees or diplomas — e.g., university computer science degree, MBA, post-graduate certificate. |

| Training | Structured learning aimed at building specific knowledge or skills — e.g., security awareness courses, certification preparation, technical product training. |

| Certification | Formal attestation of competence by a recognized authority, typically through examination — e.g., CISSP, CISM, CISA, CEH, OSCP, CDPSE, ISO 27001 Lead Auditor. |

| Experience | Demonstrated performance in roles requiring relevant competence — typically measured by years in role, complexity of work performed, and outcomes achieved. |

| Continuing Professional Education (CPE) | Ongoing learning required to maintain certifications and competence currency — typically annual hours requirement (e.g., 40 CPE/year for CISSP). |

| Awareness Program | Structured set of activities ensuring all in-scope personnel achieve and maintain awareness of ISMS requirements relevant to their roles. Includes training, communications, reinforcement, and assessment elements. |

| Targeted Audience | Defined group of personnel for whom specific awareness or training content is intended — e.g., 'all employees', 'developers', 'finance staff', 'executives', 'AI engineering teams', 'customer service personnel'. |

| Communication Plan | Documented framework specifying internal and external information security communications: what is communicated, when, by whom, to whom, through what mechanism. Required by ISO 27001:2022 Clause 7.4. |

| Internal Communication | Communications within BFS — between functions, between management and staff, between security and business units, between executive leadership and the Board. |

| External Communication | Communications with parties outside BFS — customers, regulators (BSP, NPC, SEC), business partners, vendors, media, the public, certification bodies. |

| Crisis Communication | Communications during significant security incidents requiring coordinated, accurate, timely messaging to multiple stakeholders simultaneously. Governed by BFS-ISP-017 §5.7. |

| Competence Gap | The difference between required competence for a role and current competence of the person filling it. Gaps may exist temporarily during onboarding, may emerge through role evolution, or may persist requiring development action. |

# 3.  COMPETENCE FRAMEWORK (ISO/IEC 27001:2022 CLAUSE 7.2)

This section establishes the BFS competence framework for personnel doing work that affects ISMS performance. The framework specifies required competencies for each ISMS role, methods of competence verification, gap closure mechanisms, and documentation requirements supporting ISO 27001 Clause 7.2 compliance.

## 3.1  Competence Framework Foundation

BFS adopts a structured competency framework aligned with internationally recognized models:

NIST NICE Cybersecurity Workforce Framework (SP 800-181) — comprehensive taxonomy of cybersecurity work roles, knowledge, skills, and tasks. BFS uses NICE work role categories as the foundation for ISMS role competency definitions;

ISC2 Common Body of Knowledge (CBK) for CISSP-aligned competencies in security architecture, governance, and operations;

ISACA Cybersecurity Skills Roadmap for CISM/CISA-aligned competencies in security management and audit;

Offensive Security Certified Professional (OSCP/OSEP) and CREST competencies for offensive security roles;

ISO 27001 Lead Implementer / Lead Auditor competencies for ISMS-specific roles.

Required competencies are categorized into three dimensions per the standard competency model:

| Dimension | Description | Verification Methods |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Knowledge | What the role-holder must know — concepts, frameworks, regulations, technologies, threats, BFS-specific context | Education credentials; certification examinations; structured assessments; on-the-job demonstration through performance |

| Skills | What the role-holder must be able to do — specific technical and analytical capabilities | Practical examinations (e.g., OSCP penetration testing exam); demonstrated work outputs; capstone projects; supervised performance |

| Behavioral Attributes | How the role-holder must apply knowledge and skills — judgment, communication, ethics, accountability, learning orientation | Performance reviews; 360-degree feedback; behavioral interview assessment; demonstrated decision-making in role |

## 3.3  Competence Acquisition and Maintenance

BFS uses multiple mechanisms to acquire and maintain required competence — applied in combination based on role and individual circumstances:

### 3.3.1  Pre-Employment Competence Verification

Job descriptions specify required competencies for each ISMS role per Section 3.2;

Recruitment screening assesses candidate competence against job description requirements through CV review, technical assessments (where applicable), reference checks, and structured interviews;

Pre-employment screening per the forthcoming BFS-ISP-021 (HR Security Policy) verifies background appropriate to role sensitivity;

Probationary period allows BFS to confirm competence through demonstrated performance before confirming employment.

### 3.3.2  On-the-Job Competence Development

Onboarding programs provide foundational ISMS knowledge and BFS-specific context — every new hire (regardless of role) completes BFS Information Security Onboarding within 14 days of start;

Role-specific onboarding for ISMS roles includes mentoring, supervised work, and graduated responsibility — typically 90 days before independent operation;

Continuing performance review identifies competence gaps emerging over time and triggers development action;

Stretch assignments and cross-training build broader competence across ISMS functions, reducing key-person risk.

### 3.3.3  Formal Training and Certification

BFS sponsors role-relevant certification training and examination — CISSP, CISM, OSCP, CCSP, AWS Security Specialty, and others appropriate to specific roles;

Certification maintenance through CPE is supported through internal training opportunities, conference attendance, and external course funding;

BFS maintains standing budget allocation for certification development as recognition that certifications are not optional luxuries but required competence verification mechanisms;

Specialized training in emerging areas (AI security, cloud-native attacks, post-quantum cryptography) is provided as needed to maintain currency.

### 3.3.4  Continuing Education Requirements

| Role Category | Annual CPE Hours | Sources |

| --- | --- | --- |

## 3.4  Competence Records

Per ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clause 7.2, BFS shall retain documented information as evidence of competence. The following records are maintained for all ISMS personnel:

Personnel file held by HR — including credentials, certifications, education records, employment history, performance reviews;

Competence assessment records — initial assessment at hiring, periodic re-assessment, gap analysis outputs;

Training records — training completed, certifications achieved/maintained, CPE hours accumulated;

Role-specific competence verification — where role requires specific certification, evidence of current certification status maintained;

Development plans — for personnel with identified gaps, the documented development plan including target competencies, learning activities, target completion dates, and progress tracking.

Competence records are classified per BFS-ISMS-006 §3.4 (typically INTERNAL for general training records, CONFIDENTIAL for performance reviews) and retained per the retention schedule. Records support: ISO 27001 audit verification of Clause 7.2 compliance; HR succession planning; development decisions; promotion and role assignment justification; regulatory examination evidence (BSP fit-and-proper, NPC DPO competence).

## 3.5  Competence Gap Closure

Where competence gaps are identified — through assessment, performance review, role evolution, or external assessment — BFS takes structured action to close the gap:

Gap Identification: Specific competence deficiencies are identified and documented in the personnel record;

Gap Analysis: The gap is analyzed for: severity (does it materially affect role performance?); urgency (is immediate closure needed or is gradual development acceptable?); root cause (lack of opportunity, evolving requirements, role-fit issue);

Development Plan: Documented plan with specific learning activities, target competence outcomes, timeline, accountability, and progress checkpoints;

Implementation: Personnel pursues development activities with manager support and CISO Office tracking for ISMS-specialized development;

Verification: Upon completion, competence is verified through demonstrated performance, certification achievement, or formal assessment;

Records Update: Competence records updated; development plan closed or extended as appropriate.

Where competence gaps cannot be closed within reasonable timeline — typically because the person lacks aptitude for the role or organizational support is inadequate — alternative actions include role reassignment, supplemental support (e.g., partnering junior personnel with experienced mentors), augmented external resources (consultants, MSSPs), or in extreme cases termination per HR processes.

# 4.  AWARENESS FRAMEWORK (ISO/IEC 27001:2022 CLAUSE 7.3)

This section establishes the BFS framework for ensuring all personnel under BFS control are appropriately aware of the ISMS and their responsibilities within it. ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clause 7.3 specifies four awareness elements that all in-scope personnel must achieve. The operational delivery of the awareness program is governed by BFS-ISP-014 (Security Awareness and Training Policy); this section addresses the management-system framework requirements.

## 4.1  Mandatory Awareness Elements (Clause 7.3)

Per ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clause 7.3, all persons doing work under BFS control shall be aware of:

| Clause | Mandatory Awareness Element | How BFS Achieves Awareness |

| --- | --- | --- |

| 7.3(a) | The information security policy | All personnel are introduced to BFS-ISMS-001 (Top-Level Policy) during onboarding. The Top-Level Policy is classified PUBLIC and freely available on BFS portal. Annual training reinforces key elements. Policy summaries appear in employee handbooks and contractor guidance. |

| 7.3(b) | Their contribution to the effectiveness of the ISMS, including the benefits of improved information security performance | Role-specific training (per BFS-ISP-014 §5.4) addresses how each role contributes — finance staff to BEC prevention, developers to SSDLC effectiveness, customer service to social engineering recognition, etc. Personal contribution framing in awareness messaging — 'You are part of BFS's defense'. |

| 7.3(c) | The implications of not conforming with the information security management system requirements | Disciplinary framework (per BFS-ISMS-001 §11) is communicated during onboarding. Annual training includes consequences of nonconformity. Specific examples of disciplinary outcomes (anonymized) shared periodically as deterrent without naming individuals. Customer and regulatory consequences of breaches discussed. |

| 7.3(d) | [Implicit in 27001:2022] The benefits of improved information security performance | Awareness messaging emphasizes the positive — protecting customers, preserving BFS reputation, enabling secure innovation, regulatory standing, individual and team success. Not solely fear-based messaging; balanced with positive framing of why information security matters. |

## 4.2  Awareness Program Structure

BFS awareness program operates through a layered structure ensuring appropriate depth of awareness for different audiences. The detailed program is implemented per BFS-ISP-014; this section establishes the framework requirements.

| Awareness Layer | Audience | Depth | Frequency |

| --- | --- | --- | --- |

| Foundational | All BFS personnel (employees, contractors, temporary staff) | Awareness of BFS-ISMS-001, common threats, basic protections, reporting obligations | Onboarding within 14 days; annual refresher |

| Role-Based | Personnel in roles with specific information security responsibilities (developers, finance, IT, customer service, executives, AI engineers) | Deeper awareness aligned with role-specific risks and obligations | Annual role-specific training; targeted communications throughout year |

| Specialized | ISMS specialists (per Section 3.2) — security engineers, analysts, architects, etc. | Expert-level awareness combined with deep technical competence per Section 3 | Continuous through CPE; specialized training as needed |

| Executive | Executive leadership and Board members | Strategic awareness — emerging threats, regulatory landscape, peer institution incidents, cyber risk implications | Quarterly Board cyber briefings; semi-annual executive briefings; ad-hoc on material events |

| Crisis-Triggered | Personnel affected by specific incidents or threats | Targeted awareness about specific recent threat (e.g., active phishing campaign, sector-specific incident) | As needed in response to events |

## 4.3  Awareness Content Standards

All BFS awareness content shall meet the following standards regardless of layer:

Specificity: Content addresses specific threats, controls, or behaviors rather than abstract principles. 'Use strong passwords' is generic; 'Use MFA — even if your password is stolen, the attacker cannot complete login without your second factor' is specific;

Relevance: Content reflects current threats facing BFS — financial sector phishing campaigns, ATM attacks, BEC techniques, AI-specific threats — not generic content unrelated to BFS context;

Actionability: Content provides clear actions personnel should take. Awareness without action is theater; awareness must translate to behavior;

Behavioral Realism: Content acknowledges the reality of human behavior — busy schedules, social pressure, momentary lapses — and provides defenses that work in those conditions;

Cultural Appropriateness: Content reflects Philippine financial sector context, BFS-specific scenarios, and language accessibility (English with Filipino availability where appropriate);

Currency: Content is updated as threats evolve. The phishing examples used in 2025 are not adequate for 2026; AI-enabled deepfake threats require addressing in 2026 awareness.

## 4.4  Awareness Reinforcement

Awareness is not achieved through annual training alone — sustained awareness requires reinforcement throughout the year:

Monthly phishing simulations per BFS-ISP-014 §5.7 — ongoing exercise of recognition skills;

Targeted communications when specific threats emerge — e.g., ongoing BEC campaign in financial sector triggers specific BEC reminder communication;

Visible reminders — security messaging in office spaces, login banners, periodic notification screens;

Regular security newsletter (monthly) covering recent BFS-relevant threats, control updates, and awareness reinforcement;

Lessons learned communications after CRITICAL incidents — anonymized, focused on the lesson rather than the person, building institutional knowledge;

Recognition of good security behaviors — celebrating personnel who report suspicious activity, identify vulnerabilities, or otherwise contribute to security.

## 4.5  Awareness Effectiveness Measurement

Awareness program effectiveness is measured through multiple complementary metrics — recognizing that no single metric captures awareness completely:

| Metric Category | Specific Metrics | Interpretation |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Completion | Training completion rate; certification rate; on-time completion | Direct measure of program participation; precondition for awareness but does not guarantee awareness |

| Knowledge | Post-training quiz scores; periodic knowledge spot-checks | Measure of acquired knowledge; should improve over course of program |

| Behavior | Phishing simulation click rate; phishing reporting rate; suspicious activity reporting volume; password reset frequency suggesting forgetfulness vs. compromise | Measure of behavioral awareness — most important indicator; behavior is what matters |

| Culture | Annual security culture survey; pulse surveys; employee feedback during awareness sessions | Measure of perceived organizational commitment to security; correlation with sustained behavior |

| Outcomes | Reduction in human-error-driven incidents; reduction in successful BEC attempts; reduction in social engineering incidents | Lagging indicator that awareness program is contributing to risk reduction |

BFS targets per BFS-ISMS-005 operational objectives O5.1 through O5.5: 100% mandatory training completion (O5.1); ≤5% phishing simulation click rate (O5.2); ≥30% YoY increase in security event reporting from non-security staff (O5.3); 100% role-based training completion per role (O5.4); ≥4.0/5.0 security culture survey score (O5.5). Quarterly tracking against these targets is reported to the Board Audit & Risk Committee.

## 4.6  Awareness for External Parties Under BFS Control

Per ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clause 7.3, awareness obligations apply to 'persons doing work under the organization's control' — which includes external parties operating within BFS's authority. BFS extends awareness obligations to:

Contractors and temporary staff: Same awareness program as employees; contract terms include awareness compliance obligations;

Service provider personnel with regular BFS systems access: BFS awareness training required as part of access provisioning, particularly for managed service personnel embedded in BFS operations;

Vendor personnel during specific engagements (consultants, auditors, penetration testers): Briefed on relevant BFS security expectations during engagement onboarding; ROE for penetration testing per BFS-ISP-018 includes confidentiality and security obligations;

Visitors to BFS facilities: Brief security briefing during visitor registration covering acceptable use, confidentiality, and emergency procedures;

Customers (limited scope): General awareness messaging through customer communications — secure account practices, fraud avoidance, recognizing BFS official communication. Customer awareness is not the same as personnel awareness; customers are not 'doing work under BFS control' but are stakeholders whose awareness affects mutual security.

# 5.  COMMUNICATION PLAN (ISO/IEC 27001:2022 CLAUSE 7.4)

This section establishes the BFS Information Security Communication Plan fulfilling ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Clause 7.4. The plan determines: what BFS communicates about information security; when communication occurs; with whom communication is conducted; who communicates; and the processes by which communication is achieved. The plan covers both internal and external communications across the full range of ISMS operational contexts.

## 5.1  Communication Plan Principles

The BFS Information Security Communication Plan is governed by the following principles:

Audience Appropriateness: Communications are calibrated to audience knowledge, role, and information needs. Technical detail appropriate for SOC analysts is inappropriate for general staff; high-level Board reporting differs from operational team briefings;

Accuracy: Communications are factually accurate. Speculation, premature conclusions, and unverified information are avoided. When uncertainty exists, it is acknowledged rather than concealed;

Timeliness: Communications occur at the right time — not too early when facts are incomplete, not too late when delay erodes trust or violates obligations. Regulatory clocks (BSP 2-hour, NPC 72-hour) are respected;

Proportionality: Volume and frequency are calibrated to need. Excessive communication degrades attention; insufficient communication leaves stakeholders uninformed;

Consistency: Messages communicated to multiple audiences are consistent in substance even if calibrated in detail. Different stakeholders should not receive substantively different versions of the same event;

Confidentiality: Communications respect the classification of information and the legitimate confidentiality interests of BFS, customers, and other parties. Information security communications can themselves be sensitive;

Two-Way: Communication is not just transmission — feedback channels enable recipients to ask questions, raise concerns, and contribute information. One-way communication misses critical inputs.

## 5.5  Crisis Communication

Crisis communication during significant security incidents requires coordinated, accurate, timely messaging to multiple stakeholders simultaneously. BFS-ISP-017 §5.7 specifies the operational crisis communication procedure during specific incidents. This document establishes the management-system framework.

Crisis Communication Lead: For CRITICAL/HIGH incidents, the CISO designates a Crisis Communication Lead — typically the Head of Communications or designated communications professional working in close coordination with CISO, Legal, and Incident Commander;

Pre-Approved Templates: BFS-ISP-017 maintains pre-approved communication templates for common incident scenarios (data breach, ransomware, BEC) — pre-drafting reduces time-to-communication and prevents errors during crisis pressure;

Stakeholder Mapping: Crisis communications address all relevant stakeholders systematically — customers, regulators, employees, vendors, partners, media, public — with prioritization based on legal obligations (regulatory clocks first), then customer impact, then broader stakeholders;

Single Source of Truth: During active incidents, all external communications flow through the Crisis Communication Lead to ensure consistency. Individual personnel do not communicate independently with media, regulators, or other external parties;

Executive Spokesperson: The CEO is the default external spokesperson for material incidents. Substitution by another C-level executive (typically CIO or Communications) is permitted with CEO approval. Below-C-level personnel do not act as spokesperson;

Documentation: All crisis communications are documented — drafts, approvals, distribution records — both to support post-incident review and to provide audit/legal trail.

## 5.6  Two-Way Communication Channels

Effective communication requires inbound channels — recipients providing input, asking questions, raising concerns, reporting incidents:

Security Event Reporting Hotline: 24/7 channel for personnel to report suspected security events. Single phone number and email address. Anonymous reporting permitted;

CISO Office Inbox: Channel for non-urgent inquiries, suggestions, policy questions;

Whistleblower Channel: Per BFS-ISMS-001 §11.3, confidential channel for reporting suspected violations. Information security violations can be reported through this channel;

Manager Escalation Path: Personnel can raise security concerns through their management chain;

Information Security Steering Committee: Cross-functional forum for raising and resolving information security issues;

Annual Security Culture Survey: Anonymous survey collecting feedback on awareness program, communication effectiveness, perceived organizational commitment;

Awareness Session Q&A: Training sessions include time for questions and discussion — making awareness bidirectional rather than passive consumption;

Customer-Facing Channels: Customer complaints, fraud reports, and security concerns flow through Customer Experience and are escalated to CISO Office for security-relevant items.

## 5.7  Communication During Operational Continuity

Communication systems are themselves subject to availability and integrity considerations. During operational disruptions affecting normal communication channels (network outage, ransomware affecting email, cyberattack affecting BFS infrastructure), alternative channels are required:

Out-of-band channels for executive coordination: Pre-established Signal or equivalent secure messaging groups for CISO/CEO/CIO/CRO/Legal/Board Chair communication during incidents affecting BFS infrastructure;

Personal phone numbers for key personnel: Maintained in encrypted form by Crisis Management Team for use when corporate systems are unavailable;

Alternative public communication channels: Pre-established social media accounts, third-party hosted status page, and partnerships with media outlets for emergency communications when BFS website is unavailable;

Regulatory notification channels: BSP and NPC contact information maintained in offline form; courier delivery option for regulatory notifications if electronic submission unavailable;

BCM-tested communication: Annual BCM exercises include testing of alternative communication channels per BFS-ISP-011 (forthcoming BFS-ISP-011-BCP).

## 7.2  Internal Audit Verification

Internal Audit (per BFS-ISMS-008) verifies competence, awareness, and communication effectiveness through:

Sampling-based audit of competence records for ISMS personnel — verifying records are current, complete, and reflect actual qualifications;

Verification of training completion records against attendee verification (preventing 'completion theater' where completion is recorded but learning did not occur);

Spot-check of awareness through informal verification — selecting personnel and asking basic awareness questions to validate program effectiveness beyond completion rates;

Review of communication artifacts — Board reports, regulatory submissions, customer notifications — for completeness, accuracy, and timeliness;

Verification of crisis communication readiness through tabletop exercises;

Identification of competence/awareness/communication nonconformities for tracking through BFS-ISMS-010 Corrective Action.

## 7.3  Effectiveness Indicators Beyond Compliance

Compliance metrics (e.g., 100% training completion) are necessary but not sufficient indicators of effectiveness. Effectiveness is measured through outcome indicators:

Trend in human-error-driven incidents — declining trend indicates awareness translating to behavior;

Incident reporting velocity — faster reporting indicates personnel are recognizing and acting on suspicious activity;

Quality of incident reports from non-security staff — accurate, timely, useful reports indicate genuine awareness vs. checkbox compliance;

Voluntary engagement with security topics — questions in awareness sessions, requests for additional training, participation in security culture events;

Cross-functional security collaboration — business units proactively engaging CISO Office for guidance rather than encountering security as obstruction;

Leadership signaling — executive leadership consistently modeling security-conscious behaviors visibly to organization.
