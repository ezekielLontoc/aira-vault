# 1.  PURPOSE

This policy establishes the mandatory requirements, governance framework, and operational standards for building and sustaining a comprehensive security awareness and skills training program across BFS Financial Services (hereinafter "the Organization" or "BFS") — covering all employees, contractors, and third parties who interact with BFS systems and data, through to the specialized technical training required for security practitioners and IT professionals.

Every technical control in the BFS information security program has a human dependency — a person who configures it, operates it, responds to its alerts, or can inadvertently circumvent it. Phishing emails succeed because people click them. Ransomware spreads because people open attachments. Privileged accounts are misused because people share credentials. Data is leaked because people misclassify documents. Social engineering exploits because people trust. No technical control, however sophisticated, can fully compensate for a workforce that lacks the security awareness to recognize and resist the threats directed at them.

Security awareness and training is not a compliance checkbox activity. It is an ongoing organizational capability-building program that, when executed well, measurably reduces the attack surface presented by BFS's most numerous and most targeted resource: its people. This policy ensures that training is relevant, current, role-appropriate, measured for effectiveness, and continuously improved based on real threat data and behavioral metrics.

This policy directly implements CIS Controls v8, Control 14 (Security Awareness and Skills Training), encompassing all safeguards from 14.1 through 14.9. It is anchored in ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A controls governing information security awareness, education and training, employment terms, disciplinary process, responsibilities after termination, and confidentiality agreements.

The specific objectives of this policy are to:

Establish a mandatory, continuously updated Security Awareness Program that equips all BFS personnel with the knowledge and behaviors required to recognize, resist, and report security threats;

Define role-based training requirements ensuring that personnel with elevated security responsibilities — IT administrators, developers, finance and treasury staff, executives, and security practitioners — receive training proportionate to their access level and the specific threats targeting their function;

Operate a regular, metrics-driven simulated phishing and social engineering exercise program to test and improve human resilience against the most common initial-access vectors facing BFS;

Establish a skills development program for IT security professionals, ensuring BFS maintains the technical depth required to operate, defend, and improve its security program;

Integrate security awareness with BFS's employment lifecycle — from pre-employment through onboarding, ongoing employment, role changes, and offboarding;

Define measurable awareness program performance metrics and escalation mechanisms for persistent non-compliance with training obligations;

Ensure compliance with BSP Circular 1048 cybersecurity training requirements, PCI DSS v4.0 Requirement 12.6, RA 10173 Section 21 (mandatory privacy training obligation), and all applicable regulatory training mandates.

# 2.  SCOPE

## 2.1  Personnel Scope

This policy applies to all individuals whose actions could affect the security of BFS systems or data, including:

All full-time and part-time employees of BFS across all business units, departments, functions, and geographic locations;

Contractors, consultants, managed service providers, and third-party personnel with physical or logical access to BFS systems, networks, or facilities;

Board members and senior executives who have access to BFS information systems;

Temporary staff, interns, and project-based personnel during their engagement with BFS;

Third-party suppliers and vendors whose personnel work on-site at BFS premises or have remote access to BFS systems.

## 2.2  Training Program Scope

This policy governs the following training and awareness program components:

### 2.2.1  Enterprise Security Awareness Program

Mandatory annual security awareness training for all in-scope personnel;

New joiner onboarding security orientation;

Role-change security briefings;

Ongoing awareness communications — security bulletins, threat alerts, and awareness campaigns.

### 2.2.2  Phishing and Social Engineering Simulation

Quarterly simulated phishing campaigns targeting all personnel;

Targeted social engineering exercises for high-risk populations;

Simulated vishing (voice phishing) exercises for finance and executive assistants;

Just-in-time awareness delivery for simulation failures.

### 2.2.3  Role-Based Specialized Training

Finance and treasury team: BEC and wire fraud prevention;

IT administrators and engineers: privileged access security, hardening, incident response;

Software developers: OWASP secure coding, SSDLC, dependency management;

Executives and board members: executive threat briefings, BEC, deepfake risks;

Procurement and vendor management: supply chain security;

HR team: social engineering through HR channels, employment record security;

Customer-facing staff: customer account security, vishing defense, social engineering recognition.

### 2.2.4  Security Practitioner Technical Skills Development

IT Security team: certifications, advanced threat detection, incident response, threat hunting;

SOC analysts: SOC training, SIEM operation, malware analysis, forensics;

Network and infrastructure engineers: network security, cloud security, hardening;

DevSecOps: application security, CI/CD security, cloud-native security.

# 3.  DEFINITIONS

For the purposes of this policy, the following definitions apply:

| Term | Definition |

| --- | --- |

| Security Awareness | The organizational state in which personnel understand the information security threats relevant to their role, recognize indicators of attack in their daily work environment, and know the correct action to take when a threat is encountered or suspected. |

| Security Training | Structured learning activities that develop specific knowledge, skills, and behaviors required to perform security-relevant tasks correctly and securely. Training goes beyond awareness (knowing) to capability (doing). |

| Security Culture | The collective beliefs, behaviors, and norms within an organization regarding the protection of information and systems. A strong security culture is one in which secure behavior is the default, security is everyone's responsibility, and reporting security concerns is encouraged and recognized. |

| Simulated Phishing | An authorized security exercise in which employees receive realistic but harmless phishing emails designed to test their ability to recognize and report phishing attempts, without risk to BFS systems. Results are used to measure awareness levels and target training. |

| Vishing (Voice Phishing) | A social engineering attack conducted by telephone in which the attacker impersonates a trusted entity — IT support, a bank, a government authority, or a vendor — to manipulate the target into disclosing credentials, providing access, or authorizing fraudulent transactions. |

| Smishing (SMS Phishing) | A social engineering attack delivered via SMS text messages, typically containing malicious links or instructions to call a fraudulent number. |

| Business Email Compromise (BEC) | A sophisticated email-based fraud in which an attacker impersonates an executive or trusted party to manipulate employees — particularly finance and treasury staff — into authorizing unauthorized wire transfers or disclosing sensitive information. |

| Just-in-Time (JIT) Training | Targeted security training delivered immediately after a security awareness failure event — such as clicking a simulated phishing link — providing contextual, relevant education at the moment of highest receptiveness. |

| Security Awareness Program Manager | The designated individual or team responsible for designing, implementing, scheduling, and measuring the BFS security awareness and training program. |

| Phishing Click Rate | The percentage of employees who click a link or submit credentials in a simulated phishing exercise. A key KPI for measuring organizational phishing susceptibility and awareness program effectiveness. |

| Phishing Report Rate | The percentage of employees who correctly identify and report a simulated phishing email using the designated reporting mechanism. A measure of proactive security behavior beyond mere non-clicking. |

| Role-Based Training | Security training tailored to the specific threats, responsibilities, and technical environment relevant to a defined employee role or function, going beyond the general awareness content delivered to all employees. |

| Onboarding Security Orientation | A security awareness module delivered to all new employees during their induction period, covering fundamental security behaviors, BFS-specific policies, acceptable use, and security reporting procedures. |

| Security Champion | An employee in a non-security function who has received additional security training and serves as a security advocate, point of contact, and awareness ambassador within their business unit. |

| Certification | A formally recognized professional credential awarded upon completion of an examination and demonstration of competence in a defined security domain. Examples: CISSP, CISM, OSCP, CEH, CompTIA Security+, CIPP/A. |

| Threat Briefing | A concise, timely communication to a specific audience (e.g., executive team, finance team) describing a current, relevant security threat — such as an active BEC campaign targeting Philippine banks or a new ransomware group targeting financial institutions — with specific behavioral guidance. |

| Social Engineering | The use of psychological manipulation, deception, and trust exploitation to influence people into performing actions or disclosing information that advances an attacker's objectives, bypassing technical controls by targeting the human rather than the system. |

| Security Bulletin | A periodic (typically monthly or quarterly) communication to all BFS personnel summarizing recent security incidents of relevance, emerging threats, reminders of key security behaviors, and recognition of security-positive behaviors observed within BFS. |

| Training Completion Record | The documented record of each individual's completion of assigned training modules, stored in the LMS or HR system, providing evidence of compliance for regulatory examinations and internal audits. |

| LMS (Learning Management System) | The platform used to deliver, track, and report on training activities. The LMS records training assignments, completion rates, quiz scores, and completion certificates. |

# 4.  SECURITY AWARENESS PROGRAM FRAMEWORK

BFS's Security Awareness and Training Program is structured across three complementary pillars, each targeting a different dimension of organizational security capability:

| Pillar | Objective | Target Audience | Primary Mechanism |

| --- | --- | --- | --- |

| PILLAR 1: Enterprise Awareness | Build universal baseline security knowledge and behaviors across all personnel — recognize threats, report incidents, handle data correctly. | All employees, contractors, and third-party personnel with BFS access. | Annual mandatory training; ongoing bulletins; phishing simulation; JIT training. |

| PILLAR 2: Role-Based Specialization | Develop the specific security knowledge and defenses required for higher-risk roles — finance, IT, development, executives — that face targeted, sophisticated attacks. | Defined high-risk role groups: Finance/Treasury, IT staff, Developers, Executives, Customer-facing, HR, Procurement. | Role-specific modules; targeted simulation; threat briefings; annual updates. |

| PILLAR 3: Practitioner Skills Development | Build and maintain the advanced technical security skills required by the IT Security, SOC, and engineering teams who operate and defend the BFS security program. | IT Security team, SOC analysts, Network/Infrastructure engineers, DevSecOps, application security. | Professional certifications; lab-based training; CTF exercises; conference access; cross-training. |

## 4.1  Security Awareness Maturity Model

BFS targets the following security awareness maturity levels across its personnel population, measured through simulation results, training completion, and security incident attributable to human error:

| Level | Description | Behavioral Indicators | BFS Target |

| --- | --- | --- | --- |

| Level 1 Unaware | Personnel have no security awareness — they do not recognize threats, do not follow security policies, and inadvertently take risks. | Click all phishing links; share passwords; ignore security alerts. | 0% of personnel — any unaware employees are identified and remediated. |

| Level 2 Compliant | Personnel complete required training and follow explicit security rules when enforced, but do not proactively recognize threats in novel contexts. | Pass annual training test; don't share passwords when asked not to; follow policy when reminded. | Acceptable baseline; all personnel at Level 2 minimum as measured by training completion. |

| Level 3 Conscious | Personnel proactively recognize common security threats, make security-conscious decisions, and report suspicious activity without prompting. | Voluntarily report suspicious emails; question unusual requests; choose strong passwords unprompted. | BFS target for all employees — phishing report rate > 80%; < 5% click rate. |

| Level 4 Cultural | Security is a natural, automatic part of how personnel think and work. Security behaviors are intrinsic, not externally enforced. | Informally coach colleagues; raise security concerns to management; celebrate security wins. | BFS aspirational target — security champion program and security culture metrics track progress. |

# 5.  POLICY STATEMENTS

The following policy statements are mandatory and apply to all personnel within the scope of this policy.

## 5.1  Enterprise Security Awareness Training — Mandatory Requirements

PS-1.1  All BFS personnel within the scope of this policy shall complete the BFS Annual Security Awareness Training module each calendar year. The annual training shall be assigned in the LMS in the first quarter (Q1) of each year, with a completion deadline of ninety (90) days from assignment. New joiners shall complete onboarding security orientation training within their first five (5) business days of employment.

PS-1.2  Failure to complete mandatory annual security awareness training by the defined deadline shall be escalated to the individual's manager and the Human Resources team. Personnel who have not completed training within one hundred and twenty (120) days of assignment shall be subject to the HR disciplinary process as defined in Section 10.

PS-1.3  Annual security awareness training shall cover, at minimum, the following topics — updated annually to reflect the current threat landscape and lessons learned from BFS security incidents and simulation results:

Phishing, spear phishing, and BEC recognition and reporting — including current tactics observed in the financial services sector;

Password security, multi-factor authentication, and credential hygiene;

Safe email and web browsing practices — consistent with BFS-ISP-009;

Data classification and handling requirements — consistent with BFS-ISP-003;

Acceptable use of BFS systems, devices, and internet access — consistent with BFS-ISP-020;

Remote work and mobile device security;

Physical security — clear desk, screen locking, tailgating prevention, secure document disposal;

Social engineering recognition — vishing, smishing, impersonation, pretexting;

Security incident reporting — how and when to report, what to report, who to contact;

Data privacy obligations under RA 10173 — applicable to all BFS data handlers;

Consequences of security policy violations.

PS-1.4  Annual training shall include knowledge assessment. A minimum passing score of eighty percent (80%) is required. Personnel who fail the assessment on the first attempt shall be required to review the failed modules and re-sit the assessment. Personnel who fail on the second attempt shall be referred for supervised remediation training managed by the Security Awareness Program Manager.

PS-1.5  Training content shall be reviewed and updated annually by the Security Awareness Program Manager, incorporating: lessons from BFS security incidents in the prior year, current threat intelligence relevant to BFS, regulatory updates, and results from phishing simulation campaigns.

## 5.2  Onboarding Security Orientation

PS-2.1  All new employees, contractors, and third-party personnel with access to BFS systems shall complete onboarding security orientation before or within five (5) business days of first accessing BFS systems. Access to CONFIDENTIAL or CRITICAL systems shall not be granted until onboarding security orientation is confirmed complete.

PS-2.2  Onboarding security orientation shall cover: BFS security policies and acceptable use obligations, account and password security requirements, security incident reporting procedure and contact details, phishing recognition and the BFS phishing reporting mechanism, data classification and handling fundamentals, physical security requirements for BFS premises, and consequences of security violations.

PS-2.3  Third-party contractors and vendor personnel shall be required to complete BFS security orientation relevant to their access scope, or to demonstrate equivalent security awareness through their employer's program, before being granted BFS system access. Evidence of third-party security training shall be maintained by the IT Security team.

## 5.3  Phishing and Social Engineering Simulation Program

PS-3.1  BFS shall conduct simulated phishing campaigns targeting all employees and contractors at least quarterly, using scenarios designed to reflect the most current and relevant phishing techniques being deployed against Philippine financial institutions and BFS's specific threat profile.

PS-3.2  Phishing simulations shall vary in complexity, scenario type, and delivery mechanism across campaigns to prevent personnel from pattern-matching simulation emails versus genuine threats. Scenarios shall include at minimum across annual campaigns: credential harvesting links, malicious attachment delivery (simulated), CEO/executive impersonation (BEC scenario), urgent wire transfer requests, IT helpdesk credential requests, and package delivery or prize notification lures.

PS-3.3  Phishing simulation results shall be tracked per individual over a rolling twelve (12) month period. Individual results are confidential between the employee, their direct manager, and the Security Awareness Program Manager. Aggregate results by department and business unit shall be reported to the CISO and business unit heads monthly.

PS-3.4  Just-in-Time (JIT) training shall be automatically delivered to any employee who clicks a simulated phishing link or submits credentials on a simulated phishing page. JIT training shall be brief (three to five minutes), contextual to the specific simulation scenario, and delivered immediately — within the same browser session or within one (1) hour of the click event.

PS-3.5  Employees who fail three (3) or more simulated phishing campaigns within a twelve (12) month period shall be escalated to their manager and enrolled in mandatory enhanced phishing awareness training. Employees who fail five (5) or more campaigns in twelve months shall be treated as a high-risk security behavior concern and referred to HR for formal performance management process, with the CISO notified.

PS-3.6  Simulated vishing (telephone social engineering) exercises shall be conducted at least annually, targeting the finance and treasury team, executive assistants, and IT Service Desk personnel — the most common targets for telephone-based social engineering in the financial sector. Vishing exercise results shall be reported to the CISO and the heads of the targeted functions.

## 5.4  Role-Based Specialized Training

PS-4.1  Personnel in the following high-risk roles shall complete specialized, role-specific security training in addition to the annual enterprise awareness training, at the frequencies defined in Section 4.2:

Finance and Treasury team members: BEC awareness, wire transfer fraud prevention, dual-authorization compliance, and financial social engineering recognition;

IT system administrators and engineers: privileged access security, account and credential hygiene, hardening practices, change management security, and incident response procedures applicable to their function;

Software developers and DevSecOps engineers: OWASP Top 10, secure coding practices applicable to BFS technology stacks, SSDLC security gates, dependency vulnerability management, and AI-assisted development security considerations;

Customer-facing staff (branch, call center, digital channels): customer account security, vishing defense, suspicious caller recognition, and customer data handling;

Executive team and board members: executive threat briefings, BEC and deepfake video/audio fraud, personal device security, and travel security;

HR team: social engineering through HR channels (fake employee records, fraudulent payroll change requests), employment record security, and offboarding security obligations;

Procurement and vendor management: supply chain security, vendor impersonation fraud, and contract integrity.

PS-4.2  The following role-based training frequencies are mandatory:

| Role Group | Specialized Training Frequency | Key Topics |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Finance and Treasury | Semi-annual (every 6 months) | BEC; wire fraud; dual authorization; SWIFT security; social engineering recognition |

| IT System Administrators | Annual minimum; on-the-job as role evolves | Privileged access; hardening baselines; incident response; configuration management security |

| Software Developers / DevSecOps | Annual + on release of new OWASP / framework guidance | OWASP Top 10; SSDLC security gates; SCA/SAST; secure AI-assisted development |

| Executives and Board Members | Annual executive briefing + threat bulletins quarterly | BEC; deepfake fraud; personal security; executive-targeted APT campaigns |

| Customer-Facing Staff | Annual + on emerging customer fraud pattern | Vishing; suspicious caller; account takeover recognition; customer data handling |

| HR Team | Annual | HR social engineering; payroll fraud; offboarding security; employment record integrity |

| Procurement / Vendor Mgmt | Annual | Supply chain attack; vendor impersonation; invoice fraud; contract security |

| IT Security Team | Continuous — see Section 5.5 | Advanced threat analysis; incident response; threat hunting; platform certifications |

PS-4.3  Role-based training content shall be reviewed and updated at least annually and upon emergence of new attack techniques specifically targeting the covered role. The Security Awareness Program Manager, in coordination with the CISO and relevant business unit heads, is responsible for ensuring training currency.

## 5.5  Security Practitioner Technical Skills Development

PS-5.1  The CISO shall maintain an annual Security Skills Development Plan for the IT Security team, SOC, and security-relevant engineering functions, defining the required technical competencies, training objectives, target certifications, and budget for skills development. The plan shall be approved by the CIO and reviewed annually.

PS-5.2  BFS shall invest in professional development for security practitioners through: approved professional certification programs, hands-on technical training platforms (labs, capture-the-flag, range exercises), attendance at security conferences and community events, vendor training and platform certification, and structured cross-training between security sub-disciplines.

PS-5.3  The following certifications or equivalent competency levels are defined as baseline targets for security practitioner roles at BFS:

| Role | Baseline Certification Target | Advanced / Aspirational Target |

| --- | --- | --- |

| CISO | CISSP or CISM | CRISC; SABSA; CGEIT |

| IT Security Engineer / Analyst | CompTIA Security+ or equivalent | CISSP; GIAC GSEC; CISA |

| SOC Analyst (L1/L2) | CompTIA Security+ / CySA+ | GIAC GCIH; GCIA; BlueTeam Level 1 |

| SOC Analyst (L3) / Threat Hunter | GIAC GCIH or GCIA | GIAC GREM; SANS FOR508; OSCP |

| Penetration Tester / Red Team | OSCP (Offensive Security Certified Professional) | OSED; OSEP; CRTO (Certified Red Team Operator) |

| Cloud Security Engineer | AWS Security Specialty or CCSP | Google Professional Cloud Security Engineer |

| Application Security / DevSecOps | GIAC GWEB or CSSLP | BSCP (Burp Suite Certified); OSWE |

| Network Security Engineer | Cisco CCNP Security or Juniper JNCIS-SEC | GIAC GPEN; GWAPT |

| Privacy (DPO function) | CIPP/A (IAPP Asia Pacific) | CIPM; CIPT |

PS-5.4  BFS shall allocate a defined annual training budget per security practitioner, approved by the CIO as part of the annual IT budget process. The budget shall cover certification exam fees, training course costs, conference attendance, and hands-on lab platform subscriptions. The Security Awareness Program Manager and CISO shall track budget utilization and report on skills development progress quarterly.

PS-5.5  Security practitioners who attain relevant industry certifications shall be recognized within the BFS performance management framework. Maintaining current certifications (including CPE/CPD requirements) is a performance expectation for security roles with defined certification targets.

## 5.6  Security Culture and Behavioral Reinforcement

PS-6.1  Security awareness shall be reinforced throughout the year through ongoing communications and engagement mechanisms beyond the annual training module. The Security Awareness Program Manager shall maintain an annual awareness calendar covering: monthly security tips and reminders, quarterly security bulletins, targeted threat alerts for emerging campaigns, awareness campaigns aligned with international events (Cybersecurity Awareness Month, Data Privacy Week), and internal security incident retrospectives (anonymized) shared as learning opportunities.

PS-6.2  BFS shall establish a Security Champion program — a network of employees across business units who have volunteered for, or been nominated to, an additional security ambassador role. Security Champions shall receive enhanced security training, serve as first points of contact for security questions in their business unit, and facilitate the flow of security awareness within their teams. The Security Champion program shall be managed by the Security Awareness Program Manager.

PS-6.3  BFS shall operate a recognized, simple mechanism for employees to report security concerns, near-misses, and suspected incidents without fear of blame. The report rate of security concerns — a positive indicator of security culture strength — shall be tracked and reported as a KRI. Employees who report genuine security threats shall be recognized and thanked through the BFS internal communications channels.

PS-6.4  Security awareness and training performance shall be incorporated into the BFS annual performance review process. Persistent failure to complete training obligations or persistent high-risk security behaviors shall be reflected in performance evaluations. Exemplary security-conscious behavior shall be eligible for positive recognition in performance reviews.

## 5.7  Data Privacy Awareness Training

PS-7.1  All BFS personnel who collect, process, store, or have access to personal data or Sensitive Personal Information (SPI) as defined under RA 10173 shall complete data privacy awareness training at onboarding and annually thereafter. Data privacy training is a separate mandatory requirement from general security awareness training, reflecting BFS's obligations as a personal information controller under RA 10173 Section 21.

PS-7.2  Data privacy training shall cover at minimum: the key principles of RA 10173 (transparency, legitimate purpose, proportionality); the rights of data subjects and BFS's obligations to respond to them; BFS's personal data register and data classification requirements for personal data; the BFS breach notification procedure and the 72-hour NPC reporting obligation; and the individual's specific role in protecting the personal data they handle.

PS-7.3  The Data Privacy Officer shall review and approve data privacy training content annually. Data privacy training completion records shall be maintained by the DPO Office and shall be producible as evidence during NPC inspections and BSP regulatory examinations.

## 5.8  Security Training Records and Evidence

PS-8.1  Training completion records for all mandatory security and data privacy training shall be maintained in the LMS or HR information system and retained for a minimum of three (3) years. Records shall be available to Internal Audit, the CISO, the DPO, and regulators on request.

PS-8.2  Training records shall capture: employee name and employee ID, training module title and version, assignment date, completion date, assessment score (where applicable), and completion status. Aggregate training completion rates shall be reportable by business unit, role group, and training type.

PS-8.3  The Security Awareness Program Manager shall produce a Training Completion and Awareness Effectiveness Report monthly, distributed to the CISO and CHRO. The report shall include: completion rates by business unit, phishing simulation results, JIT training delivery statistics, and outstanding non-completions requiring escalation.

## 5.9  Third-Party and Supply Chain Security Awareness

PS-9.1  Third-party personnel with regular access to BFS systems or premises shall complete BFS onboarding security orientation (or an equivalent approved alternative) before access is granted. Evidence of completion shall be retained by the BFS team responsible for the third-party relationship.

PS-9.2  Contracts with third-party service providers shall include obligations requiring the provider to maintain a security awareness program for their personnel accessing BFS data or systems, and to provide BFS with evidence of training completion upon request.

PS-9.3  For high-risk third-party relationships — software development partners, managed security service providers, and cloud service providers handling CONFIDENTIAL BFS data — the CISO or IT Security team shall review and confirm the adequacy of the third party's security awareness program as part of the annual vendor security assessment defined in BFS-ISP-005.

# 6.  PROCEDURES

## 6.1  Annual Training Cycle Management Procedure

The following procedure governs the annual execution of the mandatory security awareness training cycle:

Curriculum Review (Q4 previous year): The Security Awareness Program Manager reviews the prior year's training content, simulation results, incident retrospectives, threat intelligence, and regulatory changes. Updated content is developed or procured from the BFS-approved training platform vendor.

DPO and CISO Review: Revised training content is reviewed and approved by the CISO for security accuracy and the DPO for data privacy content. Any role-based modules are reviewed by the relevant business unit head for operational accuracy.

LMS Configuration (Q1, weeks 1–2): Training modules are uploaded to the LMS. Individual assignments are generated based on role and employment status. Completion deadlines are configured (90 days from assignment). Manager notification settings are configured for non-completion tracking.

Training Launch Communications: The CHRO and Security Awareness Program Manager send a joint communication to all staff at the start of the training period explaining: the training obligation, the deadline, how to access the LMS, and who to contact for technical issues.

Progress Monitoring (ongoing): Weekly automated reports from the LMS track completion rates. The Security Awareness Program Manager reviews completion metrics weekly. Reminder communications are sent to non-completers at 45 days and 75 days from assignment.

Escalation at Deadline: On the deadline date, a list of all non-completers is generated and provided to the CHRO and respective managers for HR escalation action.

Training Cycle Close Report: Within thirty (30) days of the completion deadline, the Security Awareness Program Manager prepares the annual Training Cycle Report documenting: overall completion rate, completion rate by business unit, assessment pass/fail rates, and any patterns requiring curriculum adjustment for the following year.

## 6.2  Phishing Simulation Campaign Execution Procedure

Each quarterly phishing simulation campaign shall follow this governance procedure:

Campaign Planning: The Security Awareness Program Manager designs the campaign scenario — template, lure type, targeted population, delivery schedule, and JIT training response. The campaign plan is reviewed and approved by the CISO. The campaign is scheduled to avoid conflicts with operational freeze periods.

IT Security Allowlisting: The phishing simulation platform sending domain and IP addresses are added to the BFS email gateway allowlist to prevent the simulation emails from being blocked. This configuration is restricted to IT Security personnel only — the operational team responsible for managing the gateway shall not be informed of the specific campaign timing to prevent inadvertent tip-off to staff.

Campaign Execution: The simulation is executed per the approved schedule. The Security Awareness Program Manager monitors delivery statistics and JIT training activations in real time.

Results Collection (72 hours post-launch): Click rates, credential submission rates, reporting rates, and JIT training completion rates are collected from the simulation platform.

Individual Follow-Up: Employees who clicked are automatically enrolled in JIT training (if not already completed). The Security Awareness Program Manager reviews the list of high-risk individuals (3+ failures in 12 months) and prepares escalation notifications for their managers.

Departmental Results Reporting: Aggregate results by department are shared with respective business unit heads for awareness and targeted improvement. Individual results are not shared with managers unless the persistent failure threshold (3+ failures) has been reached.

Campaign Summary Report: The Security Awareness Program Manager prepares a campaign summary report (scenario, delivery statistics, results, trends vs. prior campaigns, JIT completions, escalations initiated) submitted to the CISO within fourteen (14) days of campaign close.

## 6.3  Security Awareness for Role Change and Offboarding

Security awareness obligations apply at key employment lifecycle transitions:

Role Change — Access Elevation: When an employee moves to a role with significantly elevated access (e.g., a standard user becoming an IT administrator, or a general business analyst joining the finance team), they shall complete the relevant role-based training module before assuming the new access. Managers are responsible for notifying the Security Awareness Program Manager of material role changes triggering additional training requirements.

Role Change — Access Reduction: When an employee moves to a role with reduced access, the access reduction is handled through the IAM process (BFS-ISP-005). No additional training trigger applies, but the offboarding of old role responsibilities shall include a reminder of confidentiality obligations for information accessed in the prior role.

Resignation / Termination — Security Briefing: As part of the offboarding process coordinated by HR, departing employees shall receive a security briefing (verbal or written checklist) covering: return of BFS assets, account access termination, prohibition on retaining or copying BFS data, ongoing confidentiality obligations, non-disclosure agreement reminders, and reporting obligations if they are aware of any security vulnerabilities or incidents.

Offboarding Documentation: The departing employee shall sign an offboarding security checklist confirming understanding of their ongoing obligations. The signed checklist shall be retained by HR for three (3) years.

## 6.4  Security Incident Triggered Training Procedure

When a security incident is attributable in whole or in part to a human awareness failure, the following procedure applies:

Incident Classification: During post-incident review, the CISO or IT Security Manager determines whether human behavior was a contributing factor — e.g., clicked a phishing link, shared credentials, mishandled data, failed to report a suspicious event.

Individual Targeted Training: The affected individual(s) are enrolled in targeted training addressing the specific behavior gap identified — not as a punitive measure, but as a remediation action. The manager is informed.

Curriculum Update Assessment: The CISO and Security Awareness Program Manager assess whether the incident reveals a gap in the general awareness curriculum — a threat scenario not covered or inadequately covered. If so, the relevant module is updated within ninety (90) days.

Awareness Communication (Anonymized): Where appropriate, an anonymized summary of the incident type and lessons learned is included in the next security bulletin, benefiting all employees from the real-world learning example.

KPI Tracking: Security incidents attributable to human awareness failure are tracked as a KRI — the number of human-error-contributing incidents per quarter. A rising trend triggers a program review.

# 7.  GUIDELINES

## 7.1  Effective Security Awareness Design Principles

Relevance is Retention: Training content directly relevant to the employee's daily work and current threat environment is significantly more effective than generic content. Each training module should connect security concepts to real examples from BFS's industry and operational context.

Short and Frequent over Long and Rare: Multiple short training touchpoints throughout the year are more effective than a single annual module. The security bulletin, threat alerts, and simulations complement annual training with continuous reinforcement.

Story and Scenario over Lecture: Case-study-based scenarios — 'What would you do if...' — build decision-making skill more effectively than factual presentation. Scenarios based on real BFS incidents (anonymized) are especially powerful.

Positive Framing: Security awareness programs that emphasize punishment for failures create fear and reduce reporting rates. Programs that recognize and celebrate positive security behaviors — reporting phishing, strong password practices, helping colleagues — build a supportive security culture.

Accessibility: Training shall be accessible on mobile devices and available in Filipino and English to maximize engagement across all employee populations.

## 7.2  Phishing Simulation Best Practices

Realistic Scenarios: The most effective simulations reflect actual phishing campaigns observed in the wild against Philippine financial institutions. The Security Awareness Program Manager shall review current threat intelligence before each campaign to incorporate real-world lures.

No Punitive Use: Simulation results shall not be used for disciplinary action, performance management, or public shaming. The purpose is measurement and improvement, not punishment. Escalation to managers is reserved for persistent high-risk behavior (3+ failures/year), framed as a development need.

Simulating Novel Formats: BFS employees may become conditioned to recognizing standard simulated phishing formats. Campaigns shall periodically use novel delivery formats — e.g., QR codes in simulated physical flyers, SMS-based simulations, or Teams/Slack-based simulations — to test readiness beyond email.

Pre-Campaign IT Preparation: IT Security shall ensure that the simulation platform is allowlisted with sufficient lead time before each campaign. Any accidental blocking of simulation emails shall be logged and reported to the Security Awareness Program Manager immediately.

## 7.3  Executive and Board Security Briefing Guidelines

Tailored Threat Context: Executive briefings shall present threats in business impact language, not technical detail. Frame risks as operational, financial, regulatory, and reputational — connecting specific attack scenarios to potential outcomes for BFS and its customers.

Deepfake Awareness: As AI-generated deepfake audio and video become increasingly sophisticated, BFS executives shall receive specific training on: how deepfake fraud works, examples of financial-sector deepfake fraud, verification procedures for urgent financial requests received via video or audio, and the BFS call-back verification protocol for out-of-band financial authorizations.

Frequency: Executive threat briefings shall be delivered at least annually in a formal setting, supplemented by quarterly threat intelligence alerts covering campaigns specifically targeting C-suite individuals and financial sector executives.

Board-Level Engagement: At least one Board Audit and Risk Committee meeting per year shall include a security awareness agenda item — reviewing BFS's security posture, awareness metrics, and any significant security incidents — ensuring Board-level security literacy is maintained.

## 7.4  Security Skills Development Program Guidelines

Hands-On Practice: For security practitioners, theoretical training must be complemented by practical application. CTF (Capture-the-Flag) competitions, lab environments, and range exercises develop skills that cannot be acquired through reading or classroom instruction alone. BFS shall provide access to at least one hands-on security training platform (e.g., Hack The Box, TryHackMe, SANS NetWars, or equivalent) for all IT Security team members.

Community Participation: Active participation in the security community — ISACA chapters, OWASP Philippines, ISC2 Philippines, FS-ISAC, and relevant information sharing groups — provides exposure to current threats, peer learning, and professional networking that supplements formal training.

Knowledge Sharing: IT Security team members who attend external training or conferences shall be expected to share key learnings with the broader team through internal briefings or published internal notes within thirty (30) days of attendance.

Skills Gap Assessment: The CISO shall conduct an annual Security Team Skills Gap Assessment comparing current team certifications and competencies against the skills required to operate BFS's security program effectively and the emerging skills needed for the BFS technology roadmap (AI-native platform, cloud expansion). Gap findings shall inform the following year's skills development plan.

# 9.  REGULATORY COMPLIANCE

## 9.1  BSP Circulars 1140 and 1048

Security awareness and training are explicitly required components of BSP's cybersecurity and IT risk management frameworks:

BSP Circular 1048 (Cybersecurity Risk Management Guidelines) requires BSP-supervised institutions to implement a cybersecurity awareness and training program that is: regular, role-appropriate, current to the threat landscape, and measured for effectiveness. This policy directly fulfills these requirements;

BSP expects institutions to provide specific cybersecurity training for personnel in high-risk roles — particularly IT staff, finance staff, and senior management — reflecting the role-based training program in Section 5.4;

BSP technology risk examinations commonly include review of training completion records and awareness program design — training records maintained per Section 5.8 shall be produced on request;

The phishing simulation program (Section 5.3) provides measurable evidence of human resilience consistent with BSP expectations for proactive cybersecurity culture development.

## 9.2  PCI DSS v4.0 Compliance

PCI DSS v4.0 Requirement 12.6 (Security Awareness Education) is directly implemented by this policy:

PCI DSS Requirement 12.6.1 — A formal security awareness program is implemented to make all personnel aware of the entity's information security policy and procedures: implemented by the Annual Security Awareness Training Program (Section 5.1);

PCI DSS Requirement 12.6.2 — The security awareness program is reviewed at least once every 12 months and updated to address new threats and vulnerabilities: implemented by the annual curriculum review in Section 6.1;

PCI DSS Requirement 12.6.3 — Personnel receive security awareness training upon hire and at least annually thereafter, and acknowledge security policies: implemented by Sections 5.1 and 5.2;

PCI DSS Requirement 12.6.3.1 — Training includes awareness of threats and vulnerabilities that could impact security of the CDE, including phishing and social engineering: implemented by the phishing simulation program and role-based training for relevant personnel.

## 9.3  Philippine Data Privacy Act — Section 21 (RA 10173)

RA 10173 Section 21 imposes a specific, mandatory training obligation on personal information controllers (PICs) and processors:

Section 21 of RA 10173 and its Implementing Rules and Regulations require that all employees, agents, and representatives of a PIC or personal information processor who are involved in the processing of personal data undergo regular trainings and seminars on data privacy. This is a specific legal obligation, not merely a best practice;

BFS's mandatory data privacy awareness training (Section 5.7) directly fulfills this legal obligation. Training completion records maintained by the DPO Office provide the evidence required for NPC inspections;

The NPC has the authority to conduct on-site inspections of BFS and request evidence of employee privacy training as part of compliance verification under RA 10173;

BFS's privacy training program covers the rights of data subjects, the obligations of BFS as a PIC, breach notification requirements, and individual roles in protecting personal data — addressing all material components of Section 21 compliance.

# 11.  EXCEPTIONS HANDLING

## 11.1  Exception Principles

Security awareness training exceptions are granted only where genuine operational or personal circumstances prevent an individual from completing training on the standard schedule — not to reduce the training obligation itself. The training requirement is not waived; it is deferred with a defined make-up deadline.

## 11.2  Exception Categories and Approval Authority

| Exception Type | Example | Max Deferral | Approval Authority |

| --- | --- | --- | --- |

| Medical / Extended Leave | Long-term sick leave or maternity/paternity leave preventing training access. | Duration of leave + 30 days on return | HR Manager |

| Operational Emergency Deferral | Critical operational role preventing training access during deadline period. | 30 days | Manager + Security Awareness PM |

| Third-Party Equivalent Training | Contractor's employer provides demonstrably equivalent security awareness training. | Valid for current year | CISO assessment and approval |

| Role-Based Training Deferral | New role assignment; relevant training not yet available in LMS. | 60 days | Security Awareness PM |

| Phishing Simulation Exclusion | Specific role or system context makes simulation participation technically inappropriate (e.g., automated monitoring accounts). | Permanent; quarterly review | CISO |

| ABSOLUTE RESTRICTIONS:  No exception may be granted that permanently exempts any employee from the annual security awareness training obligation; permanently exempts any personal data processor from annual data privacy training (RA 10173 Section 21 obligation); or exempts executive team members or board members from annual executive security briefing. These are non-negotiable regulatory and governance requirements. |

## 11.3  Exception Process

Employee or manager submits a Training Exception Request via the ITSM or HR portal, providing: training module, reason for deferral, expected completion date, and manager approval.
